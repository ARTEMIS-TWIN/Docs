# 3.10 Administration and Management

Full visualisations for this profile are available in the dedicated Google Sheets tab

This profile includes respondents involved in institutional coordination, strategic planning, organisational management, and policy-related activities connected to cultural heritage. Their responsibilities generally concern governance, resource allocation, planning, and decision-making processes rather than direct technical implementation. As a result, their relationship with digital technologies is primarily organisational and strategic, focusing on institutional adoption, coordination, and long-term sustainability.

## 3.10.1 Digital strategies, monitoring, and organisational challenges

Respondents report varying levels of institutional engagement with digital transformation processes. While some organisations already operate within structured digital strategies or formal innovation frameworks, others are still in transitional phases or lack coherent institutional planning around digital heritage technologies.

The adoption of digital policies or strategies is uneven across institutions (**Figure 40**). Several respondents indicate the existence of structured digital plans, while others report partial implementation or isolated initiatives rather than institution-wide approaches. A smaller group reports the absence of formal digital strategies altogether.

Monitoring activities are generally oriented toward institutional performance, service management, visitor engagement, or resource coordination rather than technical infrastructure monitoring. In many cases, monitoring practices remain fragmented or dependent on external providers.

The main organisational challenges concern limited funding, lack of internal expertise, institutional resistance to change, and difficulties coordinating across departments or stakeholders. Respondents also highlight the complexity of integrating innovation into existing institutional structures and workflows.

## 3.10.2 Data management, accessibility, and organisational coordination

Administration and management professionals typically interact with aggregated institutional data rather than highly specialised technical datasets. The information they use commonly includes visitor statistics, administrative records, planning documentation, financial information, and institutional reporting materials.

Data accessibility varies considerably depending on institutional structure and technological maturity. Some organisations rely on centralised systems and shared repositories, while others still operate through fragmented environments where information is dispersed across departments or external platforms.

Collaboration and information exchange are considered essential, particularly for coordination between institutions, stakeholders, and external partners. However, respondents frequently identify organisational fragmentation, inconsistent workflows, and communication barriers as persistent limitations.

## 3.10.3 Integration challenges and Digital Twin perspectives

The integration of advanced digital technologies into administrative and managerial processes remains uneven. While respondents generally recognise the strategic value of digital innovation, implementation is often slowed by organisational constraints, limited staff capacity, and uncertainty regarding long-term sustainability.

The main obstacles in adopting digital services are primarily organisational and resource-related (**Figure 41**). Lack of funding and insufficient technical expertise emerge as the most widespread barriers, followed by institutional resistance, limited staff training, and difficulties integrating new systems into existing organisational structures.

Respondents generally perceive Digital Twins as potentially valuable tools for planning, coordination, and decision-making processes. Interest focuses particularly on their capacity to support strategic management, scenario simulation, infrastructure planning, and institutional coordination.

The perceived value of Digital Twins for planning and decision-making is strongly associated with their ability to aggregate heterogeneous information into unified environments and support more informed governance processes (**Figure 42**).

## 3.10.4 Cross–analysis insights

All detailed cross–tabulations for this profile are available in the corresponding Google Sheets tab

These insights derive from comparative cross-tabulations across the profile-specific tables. The analysis focuses on relative response distributions within each row to identify structural patterns across technological groups, rather than relying on absolute counts.

· Administrative and managerial respondents consistently frame digital technologies through organisational and strategic priorities rather than technical implementation concerns.

· Institutions with more structured digital strategies also tend to report stronger coordination capacities and more positive perceptions of Digital Twin applications.

· Organisational fragmentation, funding limitations, and lack of technical expertise remain the most recurrent barriers across all institutional configurations.

· Interest in Digital Twins is primarily connected to governance functions, planning support, coordination between stakeholders, and decision-making processes rather than immersive or technical applications.

· Respondents working within organisations that already adopted formal digital policies generally express greater openness toward integrated digital infrastructures and long-term digital transformation processes.
